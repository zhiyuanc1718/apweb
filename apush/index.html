AP Exam Description

AP Exam

The exam is given within the first weeks of May. You have September to May to prepare for this exam. Receiving a passing score on the exam might give you college credits. Scores range from 1 to 5. A 3 is considered a passing score.

  • 2019 May 10 Friday Exam
  • 2020 May 8 Friday Exam

AP Exam Format

The AP U.S. History Exam is 3 hours and 15 minutes long and has a multiple choice/short answer section and a free response section. Each section is divided into two parts.

Section I — Part A: Multiple Choice | 55 Questions | 55 minutes | 40% of Exam Score

  • Questions appear in sets of 2 to 5.
  • You’ll be asked to analyze historical texts, interpretations, and evidence.
  • Primary and secondary sources, images, graphs, and maps are included.

Section I — Part B: Short Answer | 3 Questions | 40 minutes | 20% of Exam Score

  • Analyze historians’ interpretations, historical sources, and propositions about history.
  • Questions provide opportunities for you to demonstrate what you know best.
  • Some questions include texts, images, graphs, or maps.
  • You’ll have a choice between two options for the final required short- answer question, each one focusing on a different time period.
    • Question 1 (required): periods 3-8
    • Question 2 (required): periods 3-8
    • Choose between Question 3, periods 1-5, and Question 4, periods 6-9

Section II — Part A: Document Based Question | 1 Question | 60 minutes (includes a 15-minute reading period) | 25% of Exam Score

  • Assess written, quantitative, or visual materials as historical evidence.
  • Develop an argument supported by an analysis of historical evidence.
  • The document-based question will focus on topics from periods 3-8.

Section II — Part B: Long Essay Question | 1 Question | 40 minutes | 15% of Exam Score

  • Explain and analyze significant issues in U.S. history.
  • Develop an argument supported by an analysis of historical evidence.
  • You’ll select from one of three essay choices, each focusing on the same theme and skill but different time periods:
    • Option 1: periods 1-3
    • Option 2: periods 4-6
    • Option 3: periods 7-9
 

Period 1

(1607-1754)
Period 1 is roughly 5% of your exam.

Why was 1491 and 1607 chosen for the dates of this period?

1491: 1 year prior to European contact
1607: First permanent settlement in the United States : Jamestown

This period focuses on Native American life prior to European contact and interactions between Africans, Europeans and Native Americans.

There will not be an essay solely on this period, but there could be an essay that includes this period and another period instead.
Anne Hutchinson
Hutchinson preached the idea that God communicated directly to individuals instead of through the church elders, and was exiled out of Massachusetts because of this belief.
Great Awakening
Religious revivals that spreaded across the American colonies in the late 1730s and 1740s.
Virginia House of Burgesses
The elected lower house in the legislative assembly in the New World established in the Colony of Virginia in 1619.
Bacon's Rebellion
An uprising in 1676 in the Virginia Colony led by Nathaniel Bacon, first rebellion in the American colonies in which many indentured servants took part. At the end of the Rebellion, the indentured servants were suppressed and hanged.
Stono Rebellion
The largest slave uprising in the British mainland colonies prior to the American Revolution. Laws became harsher against slaves after the rebellion.
Triangle Trade
A three way system of trade during the 1600-1800s. Africa sent slaves to America, America sent Raw Materials to Europe, and Europe sent Guns and Rum to Africa.
Middle Passage
The large migration of African Americans from Africa to America during the Triangle Trade.
Mercantilism
Economic practice in which England established the colonies to provide raw materials to the Mother Country, the colonies received manufactured goods in return.
Navigation Acts
Forced the colonists to trade with England and that the colonists could only ship certain goods.
Salutary Neglect
A period from 1607-1763 in which England did not strictly enforce Parliamentary laws, which allowed the colonies to grow economically as almost independent states for many years.
Conquistadores
A conqueror, especially one of the Spanish conquerors of Mexico and Peru in the 16th century.
Bartoleme de Las Casa
Advocated for better treatment of natives and contributed to the Black Legend.

    Period 3

    (1754-1800)
    Period 3 is roughly 12% of your exam.

    Why was 1754 and 1800 chosen for the dates of this period?

    1754: Beginning of the Seven Years War
    1800: Jefferson’s election

    This period focuses on the shift of the relationship between the colonists and the British, which causes the American Revolution. The election of 1800 shows that the United States was able to create a stable government after the political party shift from Federalists to Democratic-Republican.

    Essay topics could include but are not limited to:
  • The Seven Years War as a turning point
  • The American Revolution
  • Comparing and Contrasting the Articles of Confederations and the US Constitution.
    • American Revolution
    Event were Colonists fought against the British. The result was that the Colonists won their independence from the British.
    Anti-Federalists
    Opposers of the US Constitution, they believed in individual rights for people rather than states rights and supported the ratification of the Bill of RIghts.
    Articles of Confederation
    First American constitution that established the United States as a loose confederation of states under a weak national Congress, which was not granted the power to regulate commerce or collect taxes. The Articles were replaced by a more efficient Constitution in 1787.
    Bill of Rights
    Popular term for the first ten amendments to the U.S. Constitution. The amendments secure key rights for individuals and reserve to the states all powers not explicitly said or prohibited by the Constitution.
    Declaration of Independence
    Formal pronouncement of independence drafted by Thomas Jefferson and approved by Congress. The declaration allowed Americans to appeal for foreign aid and served as an inspiration for later revolutionary movements worldwide.
    Democratic-Republicans
    This party follows a strict interpretation of the constitution and favors a small government. Their ideas were similar to Anti-Federalist
    George Washington’s Farewell Address
    George Washington's address at the end of his presidency, warning against "permanent alliances" with other nations. Washington did not oppose all alliances, but believed that the young, fledgling nation should forge allegiances only on a temporary basis, in extraordinary circumstances.
    Federalism
    A constitutional arrangement whereby power is divided between national and state governments, each of which enforces its own laws directly on its citizens and neither of which can alter the arrangement without the consent of the other.
    Federalists
    Supporters of the US Constitution, they favored a strong national government, arguing arguing that the checks and balances in the new Constitution would safeguard the people's liberties.
    French and Indian War
    Nine-year war between the British and the French in North America. It resulted in the expulsion of the French from the North American mainland and ended Salutary Neglect in the 13 colonies.
    Hamilton's Financial Plan
    Designed to pay off the U.S.'s war debts and stabilize the economy, he believed that the United States should become a leading international commercial power. His programs included the creation of the National Bank, the establishment of the U.S.'s credit rate, increased tariffs, and an excise tax on whiskey.
    Jay's Treaty
    Negotiated by Chief Justice John Jay in an effort to avoid war with Britain, the treaty included a British promise to evacuate outposts outposts on U.S. soil and pay damages for seized American vessels, in exchange for which the United States to repay pre-Revolutionary War debts and to abide by Britain's restrictive trading policies toward France.
    Proclamation of 1763
    Decree issued by Parliament in the wake of Pontiac's uprising, prohibiting settlement beyond the Appalachians. Contributed to rising resentment of British rule in the American colonies.
    Separation of Powers
    A feature of the Constitution that requires each of the three branches of government to be relatively independent of the others so that one cannot control the others.
    Shay's Rebellion
    Armed uprising of western Massachusetts debtors seeking lower taxes and an end to property foreclosures. The Rebellion showed how weak the Articles of Confederations were to control domestic issues.
    U.S. Constitution
    The Constitution sets forth the institutional structure of the U.S. government and the tasks these institutions perform. It replaced the Articles of Confederation.
    Intolerable Acts
    several acts as punishment for protests against British. They closed the Boston harbor, restricted town meetings, sent colonists/ British soldiers to Britain for unfair trials, and passed a new quartering act that allowed British authorities power to ledge in any private household.
    John Locke
    17th century English political philosopher; helped structure and organize new colonial governments; hierarchy of power
     

    Period 4

    (1800-1848)
    Period 4 is roughly 10% of your exam.

    Why was 1800 and 1848 chosen for the dates of this period?

    1800: Jefferson’s election
    1848: End of Mexican American War

    This period focuses on the Market Revolution, the increase in democracy, and several reforms inspired by the Second Great Awakening. Settlers began to expand westwards during this time period, which would increase American nationalism but would also increase American sectionalism.

    Essay topics could include but are not limited to:
  • Reform movements inspired by the 2nd Great Awakening
  • Westward expansion and impact
    • War of 1812
    A war between the U.S. and Great Britain caused by American outrage over the impressment of American sailors by the British, the British seizure of American ships, and British aid to the Indians attacking the Americans on the western frontier. This war enhanced nationalism of Americans.
    Hartford Convention
    Meeting of Federalists near the end of the War of 1812 in which the party advocated for secession. These actions were viewed as traitorous to the country and was the major factor to the downfall of the Federalist party.
    Market Revolution
    Connected small farmers and traders to larger markets through the use of newly developed infrastructure.
    Irish immigration
    Caused largely by the potato famine in Ireland. Irish immigrants came and received much discrimination due to their Catholic faith as well as exploitation in factories due to their limited skills.
    Nativism
    Inspired in part by the rapid influx of immigrants, Americans began to feel close ties to their country, therefore displaying hate and discrimination towards foreigners who entered.
    Transcendentalism
    A philosophical and literary movement of the 1800s which emphasized living a simple life while celebrating the truth in nature, emotion, and imagination.
    Henry Thoreau
    Heavily influential transcendentalist who critiques American market society in his novel Walden, where he declares that the economic drive of America leads to moral tyranny and inability to appreciate the true beauty of nature.
    Mexican-American War
    Conflict between the Mexicans and the Americans. The result of this war was the annexation of the Mexican Cession.
    Second Great Awakening
    A series of religious revivals starting in 1801, based on Methodism and Baptism. Stressed a religious philosophy of salvation through good deeds and tolerance for all Protestant sects. The revivals attracted women, Blacks, and Native Americans.
    American System
    Economic program advanced by Henry Clay that included support for a national bank, high tariffs, and internal improvements. He emphasized strong role for federal government in the economy.
    Monroe Doctrine
    A declaration by Monroe in 1823 that warned European powers to keep out of the Western Hemisphere and pledged that the United States would not intervene in the internal affairs of Europe.
    Age of Jackson
    Period marked by the belief that ordinary people should vote in elections, hold office, and do anything they had the ability to do.
    Whigs
    A party founded by Henry Clay that emerged that largely represented many of the interests of the old Federalist party..
    Missouri Compromise
    "Compromise of 1820" over the issue of slavery in Missouri. It was decided Missouri entered as a slave state and Maine entered as a free state and all states North of the 36th parallel were free states and all South were slave states.
    John Calhoun
    South Carolina Senator and Vice President to Jackson he advocate for state's rights, limited government, and nullification.
    Indian Removal Act
    Passed by Congress under the Jackson administration, this act removed all Indians east of the Mississippi to an "Indian Territory" where they would be "permanently" housed.
    Trail of Tears
    An 800-mile forced march made by the Cherokee from their homeland in Georgia to Indian Territory; resulted in the deaths of almost one-fourth of the Cherokee people
    Bank War
    Political battle between Jackson and Clay over the renewal of the U.S. Bank. Jackson vetoed the recharter and place funds in pet banks.
    Panic of 1837
    An economic uproar when Jackson was president. Many state banks received government money that had been withdrawn from the Bank of the U.S.
    Spoils System
    A system of public employment based on rewarding party loyalists and friends.
    Temperance Movement
    A social reform effort begun in the mid-1800s to encourage people to drink less alcohol.
    Nullification Crisis
    Sectional crisis during the presidency of Andrew Jackson. South Carolina threaten to secede from America because of the high tariffs.
    The Compromise Tariff of 1833
    This compromise was proposed by Henry Clay and John C. Calhoun as a resolution to the Nullification Crisis. It was adopted to gradually reduce the Tariffs found in the Tariff of 1832 and the Tariff of Abominations.
    Tariff of Abominations
    A protective tariff passed by the Congress of the United States on May 19, 1828, designed to protect industry in the northern United States. It was labeled the Tariff of Abominations by the southerners because of the effects it had on the Southern economy.
     

    Period 5

    (1844-1877)
    Period 5 is roughly 13% of your exam.

    Why was 1844 and 1877 chosen for the dates of this period?
    Note: Periods 4 and 5 have years that overlap between 1844 and 1848

    1844: Election of James K. Polk
    1877: End of Reconstruction.

    This period analyzes the causes for, and the impacts of Manifest Destiny including tensions, the Civil War and the End of Reconstruction.

    Essay topics could include but are not limited to:
  • Change and Continuity for African Americans
  • The Civil War and Reconstruction as turning points.
    • Popular Sovereignty
    Idea that the sovereign people of a given territory should decide whether to allow slavery. It was largely opposed by Northern abolitionists who feared it would promote the spread of slavery to the territories.
    Compromise of 1850
    This compromise allowed slavery to become outlawed in Washington D.C., California to be admitted as a free state, Utah and New Mexico to determine whether slavery is allowed through popular sovereignty and the Fugitive Slave Law to be ratified.
    Civil War
    Conflict between the Union and Confederates over slavery
    Fugitive Slave Act of 1852 -These laws provided for the return of escaped slaves to their owners.
    Kansas-Nebraska Act
    A compromise law in 1854 that suspended the Missouri Compromise and left it to voters in Kansas and Nebraska to determine whether they would be slave or free states.
    Bleeding Kansas
    A sequence of violent events involving abolitionists and Pro-Slavery people that took place in Kansas-Nebraska Territory. The dispute further strained the relations of the North and South, making civil war imminent.
    Abraham Lincoln
    President during the Civil War and advocated for the preservation of the Union.
    Republican Party
    This party was organized in 1854 by antislavery Whigs, Democrats, and Free Soilers in response to the passage of the Kansas-Nebraska Act.
    Know-Nothing Party
    This party mainly consisted of people that were Nativists, people that discriminated against foreigners.
    Free Soil Party
    This party mainly consisted of people that were against slavery in new territories.
    Sumner-Brooks Incident
    Charles Sumner gave a two day speech on the Senate floor. He denounced the South for crimes against Kansas and singled out Senator Andrew Brooks of South Carolina for extra abuse. Brooks beat Sumner over the head with his cane, severely crippling him.
    Dred Scott Decision
    Supreme Court decision stated these three things: Blacks were not citizens and therefore could not sue in federal courts, because a slave is their master's property, they can be taken into any territory and held there in slavery, and Congress had no power to ban slavery from the territories.
    John Brown's Raid
    John Brown seized the U.S. arsenal at Harpers Ferry and planned to end slavery by massacring slave owners and freeing their slaves. He was captured and executed.
    Manifest Destiny
    Widely held belief in the United States that its settlers were destined to expand across North America.
    Robert E. Lee
    The General of the Confederate troops during the Civil War. He surrendered to General Grant at Appomattox Court House on April 9, 1865.
    Ulysses S. Grant
    A general and political leader of the nineteenth century.
    Anaconda Plan
    Plan for civil war proposed by general-in-chief Winfield Scott, which emphasized the blockade of Southern ports and called for an advance down the Mississippi River the cut the South in two. This plan would suffocate the South.
    Emancipation Proclamation
    This proclamation made all slaves in the rebelling free.
    Gettysburg Address
    Address spoken by Lincoln in Gettysburg. In the address, Lincoln framed the war as a means to uphold the values of liberty.
    Total War
    Mobilized all of a society's resources in support of the military effort.
    Sherman's March to the Sea
    Sherman's march from Atlanta to South Carolina. He and his army applied a total warfare, scorched earth policy that led over a million dollars in damage and crushed the south.
    13th Amendment
    This amendment freed all slaves without compensation to the slave owners. It legally forbade slavery in the United States.
    Civil Rights Act
    This act pronounced all African Americans to be US citizens (overriding the Dred Scott decision), and also attempted to provide a legal shield against the operation of the southern states' Black Codes.
    Radical Republicans
    These were a small group of people in 1865 who supported black suffrage, believed the South should be harshly punished and thought that Lincoln was sometimes too compassionate towards the South.
    Reconstruction Act
    Divided the Confederate states except Tennessee into five military districts.
    14th Amendment
    This amendment granted citizenship for African Americans, Repealed the 3/5 Compromise, denied former confederate officials from holding national or state office, and rejected confederate debts.
    Black Codes
    Laws or "codes" passed in the southern states during Reconstruction that greatly limited the freedom of former slaves.
    Ku Klux Klan
    A group of mostly Southerners who were extremely racist against African Americans, and disliked all other cultures and races. They lynched African Americans.
    15th Amendment
    This gave black males the right to vote and gave congress the power to enforce this through legislation.
    Carpetbaggers
    Skilled union army vets that carried around everything they owned in cheap suitcases called carpetbags.
    Scalawags
    Ex-confederates who supported reconstruction.
    Boss Tweed
    NYC's powerful democratic political machine in 1868.
    Political machine
    A political group in which an authoritative boss or small group commands the support of a corps of supporters and businesses, who receive rewards for their efforts
    Compromise of 1877
    This compromise withdrew federal troops from the south and would make Hayes win the election of 1876 by 1 electoral vote.
    Free Soil Party
    People that were in this party were against slavery in the new territories. They also advocated federal aid for internal improvements and urged free government homesteads for settlers.
    Uncle Tom's Cabin
    Anti-slavery novel written by Harriet Beecher Stowe that fueled the abolitionist movement in the northern states
     

    Period 6

    (1865-1898)
    Period 6 is roughly 13% of your exam.

    Why was 1865 and 1898 chosen for the dates of this period?
    Note: Periods 5 and 6 have years that overlap between 1865 and 1877.

    1865: End of Civil War
    1898: Beginning of the Spanish-American War

    This period focuses on the Gilded Age, Social Darwinism, the Growth of Labor Unions, the Populist Party and US expansion out west and overseas.

    Essay topics could include but are not limited to:
  • Comparing and contrasting goals of farmers and industrial workers
  • Success and Failures of Reconstruction during this time period.
    • Laissez Faire
    Economic liberalism that believes in unrestricted private enterprise and no government interference in the economy. Also known as capitalism.
    Trust
    A large grouping of business interests with significant market power.
    Social Darwinism
    The application of ideas about evolution and "survival of the fittest" to human societies
    JP Morgan
    An influential banker and businessman who bought and reorganized companies. His US Steel company would buy Carnegie steel and become the largest business in the world in 1901.
    John D Rockefeller
    Established the Standard Oil Company, the greatest, wisest, and meanest monopoly known in history.
    Andrew Carnegie
    A business man that increased his power through by gaining control of the many different businesses that make up all phases of steel production development.
    Horizontal Integration
    A technique used by John D. Rockefeller. It is an act of joining or consolidating with one's competitors to create a monopoly.
    Vertical Integration
    Practice where a single entity controls the entire process of a product, from the raw materials to distribution.
    Robber Baron
    Robber Barons were industrialists or big business owners who gained huge profits by paying their employees extremely low wages. They also drove their competitors out of business by selling their products cheaper than it cost to produce it.
    Sherman Antitrust Act
    Signed into law by Harrison and was extensively used by Theodore Roosevelt for trust-busting. However, it was initially misused against labor unions
    Gilded Age
    Time period were the United States was becoming prosperous, despite the corrupt politics & growing gap between the rich & poor
    Knights of Labor
    Labor Union that was open to everyone but lawyers and bankers. This Union failed because of the Haymarket Square Riot.
    Great Railroad Strike of 1877
    A violent but ultimately unsuccessful interstate strike, which resulted in extensive property damage and many deaths.
    Haymarket Square Riot
    Riot led by the knights of labor in Chicago. It began as an attempt to secure an 8 hour work day. 12 people were killed when an anarchist threw a bomb into a column of policemen.
    Homestead Strike 1892
    Strike where Carnegie's steel workers had a sit down strike. In response, Carnegie hired the Pinkerton Detective Agency to stop the strike, but his attempt failed.
    Pullman Strike
    This strike began when the Pullman Palace Car company severely cut wages for its workers. The railroad workers responded by refusing to link any Pullman car to a train.
    Eugene Debs
    American union leader, one of the founders of the International Labor Union.
    American Federation of Labor
    Labor Union that was founded by Samuel Gompers. People that were in this Union were skilled workers, sought better wages, hours, working conditions, rejected socialist and communist ideas.
    Sharecropping
    A system used on southern farms after the Civil War in which farmers worked land owned by someone else in return for a small portion of the crops.
    Populists
    Group that called for nationalizing the railroads, telephones, and telegraph; income tax, loans for farmers, and free and unlimited coinage of silver. Mainly consisted of farmers.
    Free silver
    Political issue involving the unlimited coinage of silver, supported by farmers and William Jennings Bryan.
    Interstate Commerce Act
    Monitors the business operation of carriers transporting goods and people between states
    Socialism
    A system in which society, usually in the form of the government, owns and controls the means of production.
    Chinese Exclusion Act
    Denied any additional Chinese laborers to enter the country while allowing students and merchants to immigrate.
    Jim Crow Laws
    State laws in the South that legalized segregation.
    Grandfather clause
    A clause in registration laws allowing people who do not meet registration requirements to vote if they or their ancestors had voted before 1867.
    Gospel of Wealth
    This was a book written by Carnegie that described the responsibility of the rich to be philanthropists. This softened the harshness of Social Darwinism as well as promoted the idea of philanthropy.
    Social Gospel
    A movement in the late 1800s / early 1900s which emphasized charity and social responsibility as a means of salvation.
    Dawes Act
    This act assimilated the Native Americans into American Society.
    Spanish-American War
    War fought between the US and Spain in Cuba and the Philippines. It lasted less than 3 months and resulted in Cuba's "independence" as well as the US annexing Puerto Rico, Guam, and the Philippines. It was started in part by the influence of yellow journalism after the explosion and sinking of the USS Maine.
     

    Period 7

    (1890-1945)
    Period 7 is roughly 17% of your exam.

    Why was 1890 and 1945 chosen for the dates of this period?
    Note: Periods 6 and 7 have years that overlap between 1890 and 1898.

    1890: “Closing” of the Frontier
    1945: End of WWII

    This period focuses on the Gilded Age, Social Darwinism, the Growth of Labor Unions, the Populist Party, US expansion overseas, the Roaring Twenties, the Great Depression, the New Deal and US involvement in WWI and WWII.

    Essay topics could include but are not limited to:
  • Turning Points for Spanish-American War, Progressive Era, WWI, Great Depression, New Deals, and WWII
  • Changes and Continuities for Immigrants, African Americans and Foreign Policy
    • Frederick Jackson Turner
    Known for his frontier thesis, "The Significance of the Frontier in American History", he stressed the movement of the frontier as people began to migrate west. As people began to migrate west, the Frontier moved towards the west.
    Jane Addams
    Founder of Settlement House Movement and Hull House.
    Muckrakers
    A group of writers, journalists, and critics who exposed corporate wrongdoings and political corruption in the first decade of the 20th century.
    Jacob Riis
    A Danish immigrant and muckraker that became a reporter who pointed out the terrible conditions of the tenement houses of the big cities where immigrants lived during the late 1800s. He wrote How The Other Half Lives in 1890.
    18th Amendment
    Prohibited the non-medical sale of alcohol.
    21st Amendment
    Repealed the 18th Amendment, which prohibited the non-medical sale of alcohol.
    Ida Tarbell
    Investigative journalist and muckraker that wrote a report condemning the corrupt business practices of John D. Rockefeller in McClure's magazine.
    Upton Sinclair
    Sinclair was a muckraker who shocked the nation when he published The Jungle, a novel that revealed gruesome details about the meat packing industry in Chicago.
    Pure Food and Drug Act
    This act forbade the manufacture or sale of mislabeled or adulterated food or drugs.
    Theodore Roosevelt
    Roosevelt was a president, from 1901-1909 that passed two acts that purified meat, went after trusts, formed the "Bull Moose Party”, and wanted to build the Panama Canal.
    US Forest Service Act
    Act that worked to protect forests and the environment
    Margaret Sanger
    Sanger advocated birth control awareness.
    Alice Paul
    Paul believed that giving women the right to vote would eliminate the corruption in politics.
    National American Woman Suffrage Movement
    A group formed by leading suffragist in the late 1800s to organize the women's suffrage movement. Led by Elizabeth Cady Stanton.
    19th Amendment
    This amendment extended the right to vote to women in federal or state elections.
    NAACP
    Interest group for African Americans
    W.E.B. Du Bois
    He advocated immediate equal treatment and equal educational opportunities for blacks.
    Franklin Delano Roosevelt
    President from 1933 to 1945. Known for created the New Deal and was the only president to serve more than 2 terms.
    Woodrow Wilson
    President from 1913 to 1921. Known for World War I leadership, Federal Reserve, Federal Trade Commission, Clayton Antitrust Act, progressive income tax, lower tariffs, women's suffrage, Treaty of Versailles, 14 points, and League of Nations.
    Clayton Antitrust Act
    New antitrust legislation constructed to remedy deficiencies of the Sherman Antitrust Act.
    Federal Reserve Act
    A 1913 law that set up a system of federal banks and gave government the power to control the money supply.
    Recall
    A procedure for submitting to popular vote the removal of officials from office before the end of their term.
    Referendum
    A legislative act is referred for final approval to a popular vote by the electorate.
    Initiative
    A procedure by which voters can propose a law or a constitutional amendment.
    Imperialism
    A policy in which a strong nation seeks to dominate other countries politically, socially, and economically.
    Good Neighbor Policy
    FDR's foreign policy of promoting better relations with Latin America by using economic influence rather than military force in the region
    Annexation of Hawaii
    By the late 1800s, U.S. had exclusive use of Pearl Harbor. In July 1898, Congress made Hawaii a U.S. territory, for the use of the islands as naval ports.
    Open Door Policy
    A policy proposed by the US in 1899, under which ALL nations would have equal opportunities to trade in China.
    Yellow Journalism
    Journalism that exploits, distorts, or exaggerates the news to create sensations and attract readers.
    Anti-Imperialist League
    Founded on June 15, 1898 in Boston, Massachusetts, in opposition of the acquisition of the Philippines by the United States. The anti-imperialists opposed the expansion because they believed it violated the Constitution.
    Roosevelt Corollary
    Roosevelt's 1904 extension of the Monroe Doctrine, stating that the United States has the right to protect its economic interests in South and Central America by using military force, first put into effect in Dominican Republic.
    Dollar Diplomacy
    Foreign policy created under President Taft that had the U.S. exchanging financial support for the right to help countries make decisions about trade.
    Zimmerman Telegram
    Germany sent this to Mexico instructing an ambassador to convince Mexico to go to war with the U.S. It was intercepted and caused the U.S. to mobilized against Germany, which led to the U.S joining WWII.
    Liberty Bonds
    People bought bonds so the government could get that money now for war. The bonds increased in interest over time.
    Great Migration
    Movement of African Americans from the South to the North for jobs.
    Immigration Act of 1917
    Established new immigration system that restricted immigrants from migrating into the U.S.
    Espionage and Sedition Acts
    Two laws enacted to impose harsh penalties on anyone interfering with or speaking against U.S participation in WW1.
    Fourteen Points
    A peace program presented to the U.S. Congress by President Woodrow Wilson in January 1918. It called for the evacuation of German-occupied lands, the drawing of borders and the settling of territorial disputes by the self-determination of the affected populations, and the founding of an association of nations to preserve the peace and guarantee their territorial integrity.
    Treaty of Versailles
    The treaty end of WWI and demanded war reparations from the Germans.
    League of Nations
    International organization founded in 1919 to promote world peace and cooperation but greatly weakened by the refusal of the United States to join. It was ineffective in stopping aggression by Italy, Japan, and Germany during WWII.
    Flapper
    Young women of the 1920s that behaved and dressed in a radical fashion.
    Immigration Act of 1921
    This act limited the number of immigrants that could be admitted from any country to 2% of the amount of people from that country who were already living in the U.S. as of the census of 1890.
    Scopes Monkey Trial
    Trial where John Scopes was charged with violating state law by teaching Charles Darwin's theory of evolution.
    Harlem Renaissance
    A period in the 1920s when African-American achievements in art and music and literature flourished.
    Lost Generation
    Group of writers in 1920s who shared the belief that they were lost in a greedy, materialistic world that lacked moral values and often choose to flee to Europe.
    Black Tuesday
    A name given to October 29, 1929, when stock prices fell sharply and the Great Depression started.
    Bonus Army
    Facing the financial crisis of the Depression, WWI veterans tried to pressure Congress to pay them their retirement bonuses early. Angry veterans marched on Washington D.C., and Hoover called in the army to get the veterans out of there.
    Herbert Hoover
    The president of the United States from 1929 to 1932. The early years of his presidency brought about a great deal of prosperity for the US but many people hated him because they blamed him for the stock market crash.
    Hoovervilles
    Depression shantytowns, named after the president whom many blamed for their financial distress.
    Dust Bowl
    A drought in the 1930s in the Great Plains where many crops were destroyed.
    New Deal
    Relief for the people out of work, Recovery for business and the economy as a whole, and Reform of American economic institutions.
    Progressive Era
    widespread social activism and political reform across the United States that spanned from the 1890s to the 1920s.
    Great Depression
    Severe worldwide economic depression that took place mostly during 1929 to 1939.
    Bombing of Pearl Harbor
    Japan bombs Pearl Harbor, causing the US to join WWII
    Bombing of Hiroshima and Nagasaki
    The US retaliated against the Japanese by bombing Hiroshima and Nagasaki, leading to Japan surrendering WWII.
    Roaring Twenties
    A period of economic prosperity during the 1920s.
    Red Scare
    Widespread fear about a potential rise of communism in the United States. It lead to many people losing their jobs and getting arrested.
    United Nations
    An international organization created after World War II to promote international cooperation, stop wars between countries, and provide a platform for dialogue and diplomacy.
    Japanese Internment
    Carried out by president Roosevelt through Executive Order 9066, this order took many Japanese families away from their homes and into internment camp. It was motivated by racism after Pearl Harbor bombing
    Triangle Shirtwaist Company fire
    In 1911, bolted the fire escape door shut in order to keep female workers from taking breaks. A fire later broke out and killed 146 workers, a large majority were women.
     

    Period 8

    (1945-1980)
    Period 8 is roughly 15% of your exam.

    Why was 1945 and 1980 chosen for the dates of this period?

    1945: End of WWII
    1980: Election of Ronald Reagan

    This period focuses on the Cold War, Civil Rights, Gay Rights, Women’s Rights, The Great Society, Political Scandals, Controversies (Watergate and the Middle East) and the US reaction towards the rising threat of the Soviet Union and Communism.

    Essay topics could include but are not limited to:
  • US foreign policy
  • Civil Rights
    • Communism
    A government system created by Karl Marx advocating class war and leading to a society in which all property is publicly owned.
    Yalta Conference
    Meeting between Churchill, Stalin, and Roosevelt after WWII in which the leaders discussed plans for the post-war world and Germany.
    Containment
    A policy based in the belief that if communism could be kept within its existing boundaries, it would collapse under the weight of its internal weaknesses.
    Truman Doctrine
    Doctrine made by Truman in 1947 that was a policy of providing economic and military aid to any country threatened by communism or totalitarian ideology, mainly helped Greece and Turkey.
    Marshall Plan
    A plan that the US came up with to revive war-torn economies of Europe. This plan offered $13 billion in aid to western and Southern Europe.
    Berlin Airlift
    The Airlift supplied food and fuel to citizens of West Berlin when the Russians closed off land access to Berlin.
    NATO
    The First peacetime alliance the United States belonged to. This Alliance was made in response to the Warsaw Pact It made a promise to defend one another if they were attacked by any other country
    USSR
    A superpower during the Cold War.
    Ho Chi Minh
    Vietnamese leader who is responsible for ousting first the French, then the United States from his country. Supported by both communist China and the Soviet Union he achieved his goal of a united communist Vietnam in 1975
    Bay of Pigs
    An unsuccessful invasion of Cuba in 1961 that was sponsored by the United States. Its purpose was to overthrow Cuban dictator Fidel Castro.
    Cuban Missile Crisis
    A Brink-of-war confrontation between the United States and the Soviet Union over the placement of nuclear-armed missiles in Cuba during the Cold War. In the end, The Soviet Union agreed to remove the missiles from Cuba if the U.S. removed missiles from Turkey and that the U.S. would not invade Cuba.
    Gulf of Tonkin Resolution
    Congressional resolution that authorized President Johnson to commit US troops to South Vietnam and fight a war against North Vietnam during the Cold War.
    Tet Offensive
    The Vietcong and North Vietnamese forces launched a huge attack on the Vietnamese New Year in South Vietnam. The U.S. won the battles but Americans reacted sharply, with declining approval of Johnson and more anti-war sentiment.
    Détente
    A lessening of tensions between U.S. and Soviet Union. Besides disarming missiles to insure a lasting peace between superpowers, Nixon pressed for trade relations and a limited military budget.
    SALT
    Stands for Strategic Arms Limitation Talks. This was a pact that served to freeze the numbers of long-range nuclear missiles for five years in 1972.
    Mikhail Gorbachev
    Head of the Soviet Union from 1985 to 1991. His liberalization effort improved relations with the West, but he lost power after his reforms led to the collapse of Communist governments in eastern Europe.
    Little Rock Nine
    Governor Faubus sent the Arkansas National Guard to prevent nine Black students from entering Little Rock Central High School. Eisenhower sent in U.S. paratroopers to ensure the students could attend class.
    Martin Luther King, Jr.
    Leader of the Southern Christian Leadership Conference (SCLC) believed in nonviolent civil disobedience. He gave his most famous speech, "I have a dream” in 1963.
    Sit Ins
    Non violent protest where people sit at lunch counters and refuse to leave until served.
    Great Society
    This was part of President Johnson's war on poverty. In 1965, Congress passed many Great Society measures, including Medicare, civil rights legislation, and federal aid to education.
    Civil Rights Act
    This act made racial, religious, and sex discrimination by employers illegal and gave the government the power to enforce all laws governing civil rights, including desegregation of schools and public places. This act was ratified in 1964.
    Fair Deal
    An economic extension of the New Deal proposed by Harry Truman that called for higher minimum wage, housing and full employment.
    New Frontier
    President Kennedy's 1960 plan aimed at improving the economy, fighting racial discrimination, and exploring space.
    Watergate scandal
    Scandal in the Nixon administration that began with break-in at the Democratic National Committee headquarters at the Watergate Office Building in Washington, D.C. President Nixon's role in the cover-up of justice led to his resignation in 1974.
    Cold War
    A war of words and threats between the United States and the Soviet Union that was marked primarily by a political and economic, rather than military, struggle between the two nations.
    Reaganomics
    This was a "trickle down" theory or supply side economic policies. Reagan believed that by cutting federal spending on domestic programs and cutting taxes for the wealthy and corporations, he would help create new jobs.
    Ronald W. Reagan
    President of the United States from 1981 to 1989. While president, he cut out many welfare and public works programs. He used the Strategic Defense Initiative to avoid conflict. His meetings with Gorbachev were the first steps to ending the Cold War.
    Baby Boom
    An increase in population by almost 30 million people due to an influx of birth. This spurred a growth in suburbs and three to four children families.
    Immigration Act of 1965
    Abolished the national-origins quotas and providing for the admission each year of 170,000 immigrants from the Eastern Hemisphere and 120,000 from the Western Hemisphere.
    Nixon Doctrine
    President Nixon's policy of requiring countries threatened by communism to assume most of the military burden, with the United States offering political and economic support.
    Julius and Ethel Rosenberg
    Husband and wife who were executed in 1953 for passing information about the atomic bomb to the Soviets.
    McCarthyism
    Term used to describe the tactic of making accusations of corruption or disloyalty without evidence and no regard for civil liberties.
    Joseph McCarthy
    Republican Senator from Wisconsin who in 1950 began a Communist witch-hunt that lasted until he was censured by the Senate in 1954.
    Vietcong
    Vietnamese Communist rebels in South Vietnam.
    Vietnamization
    President Nixon's policy of gradually removing U.S. troops from Vietnam.
    Kent State Shooting
    University in Ohio where students were shot and killed by National Guard Troops during a protest against the Vietnam War.
    Eisenhower Doctrine
    Policy formulated by President Eisenhower of providing military and economic aid to Arab nations in the Middle East to help defeat Communist-nationalistic rebellions.
    Richard Nixon
    President of the United States in 1968 and 1972. He was responsible for getting the United States out of the Vietnam War by using "Vietnamization", which was the withdrawal of 540,000 troops from South Vietnam for an extended period. He was the first President to ever resign, due to the Watergate scandal.
    Arms Race
    A period of high tension between the Soviet Union and the United States where the goal was only to have more nuclear weapons than the opponent. Neither the USSR or US won
    Space Race
    A competition of space exploration between the US and USSR. This investment led to great scientific advances, but also caused strong tensions between both countries.
    Vietnam War
    The prolonged struggle between nationalist forces attempting to unify the country of Vietnam under a communist government and the US attempting to prevent the spread of communism. In the end, Vietnam was unified as a whole communist country.
    Massive Retaliation
    Eisenhower's policy of advocating the full use of American nuclear weapons to counteract a Soviet ground attack in Europe
     

    Period 9

    (1980-Present day)
    Period 9 is roughly 5% of your exam.

    Why was 1980 and Present day chosen for the dates of this period?

    1980: Election of Ronald Reagan
    Present: It’s today!

    This period focuses on the end of the Cold War, Ronald Reagan, Conservative movement, and Terrorism

    There will not be an essay solely on this period, but there could be an essay that includes this period and another period instead.
    Stagflation
    A period of falling output and rising prices.
    Iranian hostage crisis
    Kidnapping of American Embassy hostages in Iran. The Crisis lasted for more than a year.
    Economic Recovery Tax Act
    A federal law passed to boost the economy, reduce inflation and increase employment. This act was ratified in 1981.
    Fall of Berlin Wall
    This event in 1989 marked the symbolic end of the Cold War and was the beginning of the collapse of communism in eastern Europe.
    Persian Gulf War
    Began when Saddam Hussein, President of Iraq, invaded the oil-rich country of Kuwait. This belligerent action threatened to disturb the world's oil supply. The UN responded with a trade embargo against Iraq, and eventually with an multinational military force that made Iraq withdraw from Kuwait.
    Immigration Act of 1990
    Abolished the national-origins quotas and providing for the admission each year of 170,000 immigrants from the Eastern Hemisphere and 120,000 from the Western Hemisphere.
    Barack Obama
    First African American to become president. He enacted education reform in his Race to the Top program and managed to pass healthcare reform in the Affordable Care Act, or Obamacare.
    Clinton–Lewinsky scandal
    Clinton was almost impeached for perjury after an affair with White House intern, Monica Lewinsky. Although denying any sexual relations, leaked tapes proved this to be false. The House had enough votes to impeach, but the Senate did not so he was acquitted of charges and finished his term
    NAFTA
    Stands for the North American Free Trade Agreement. This established free trade zone between Canada, United States and Mexico. There was a net gain in jobs due to opening of Mexican markets.
    Conservatism
    A political philosophy based on tradition and social stability, favoring obedience to political authority and organized religion.
    Iraq war
    The USA went to war with Saddam Hussein because Hussein kicked out UN weapons inspectors, he was building weapons of mass destruction, he didn't obey the no-fly zones limitations and he was a savage dictator.
    Fall of the Berlin Wall
    Beginning of the fall of communism and the Soviet Union. The fall of the berlin wall symbolized the failure of communism and massive socialism, which ultimately led to the end of the Cold War and fall of the Soviet Union.
     

    Important Court Cases to know on the AP exam :

    Marbury v. Madison (1803). → Established judicial review.
    Fletcher v. Peck (1810). → Upholds the sanctity of contracts.
    McCulloch v. Maryland (1819). → Bank of the United States is constitutional.
    Dartmouth College v. Woodward (1819). → Upholds the sanctity of contracts.
    Gibbons v. Ogden (1824). → Congress has power over interstate commerce.
    Cherokee Nation v. Georgia (1831). → Tribes are not sovereign nations.
    Worcester v. Georgia (1832). → Tribes are sovereign and have autonomy.
    Commonwealth v. Hunt (1842). → Labor unions were lawful.
    Dred Scott v. Sanford (1857). → Scott is not a citizen and can’t sue.
    Ex Parte Milligan (1866). → Federal government could not establish military courts to try civilians
    Munn v. Illinois (1877). → States can regulate businesses in public interest.
    Wabash Case (1886). → Granger laws regulating interstate trade are illegal.
    Plessy v. Ferguson (1896). → Segregation is legal.
    Insular Cases (1901). → Territories are not given Constitutional rights.
    Northern Securities Co. v. U. S. (1904). → Government can battle trusts.
    Muller v. Oregon (1908). → 10 hour workday for women.
    Schenck v. U.S. (1919). → 1st Amendment limited during WWI.
    Adkins v. Children’s Hospital (1923). → Minimum wage for women is illegal.
    Schechter v. U. S. (1936). → NRA under the New Deal is unconstitutional.
    Korematsu v. U. S. (1941). → Japanese internment is upheld.
    Brown v. Board of Education (1954) → Segregation is not legal.
    Engel v. Vitale (1962). → Schools can’t force students to say prayers.
    Miranda v. Arizona (1966). → Gave people the right to remain silent.
    Roe v. Wade (1973). → Right to abortion and privacy.
    U.S. v. Nixon (1974). → Nixon can’t claim executive power over tape release.
    Clinton v. Jones (1997) → President isn’t immune from lawsuit while in office.
    Bush v. Gore (2000). → Bush wins the election.
    Obergefell v. Hodges (2016). → Legalized same-sex marriage nationwide.
